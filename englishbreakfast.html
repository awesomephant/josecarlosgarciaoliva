- Mushroom

Mushroom etymologically come from Old French mousseron, from late Latin mussirio. Mushroom is a fungal (fungus is borrowed from Latin fungus) growth that typically takes the form of a domed cap on a stalk. If we go back in time, the Pharaohs decreed that no-one except them could eat mushrooms then, it became already a part of the Egyptians’s dishes…

- Bacon

Bacon means ‘meat from the back and sides of a hog’ (either fresh or cured, but especially cured), this word come originally from Old French bacun, that come from Proto-Germanic bakkon…

- Eggs

In China, birds are described as "the animal that knows time", probably due to the reliability of the so-called morning rooster and the regularity of chicken egg production. With respect to eggs, this word comes from the old English term that was oeg, which survived in middle English as ey (plural eyren). But this word was in turn borrowed during the fourteenth century from the old Norse…

- Baked Beans

The beans that are currently used to make baked beans are all native to South America, with two geographical origins: Mesoamerica and Los Andes. It is known that they have been part of the culture of Latin America for approximately 8,000 years…

- Sausage

Nowadays, is an important part of the cuisine in different countries in Africa, Asia, Europe, North America, South America and Oceania. The modern word "sausage" is derived from the Late Middle English from Old Northern French saussiche, from medieval Latin salsicia, from Latin salsus, meaning something that has been salted…

- Black Pudding

The first known written mention of black pudding was as early as 800 BC when it appeared in Homer’s classic The Odyssey. In book twenty of his great canon, Homer wrote: “As when a man besides a great fire has filled a sausage with fat and blood and turns it this way and that and is very eager to get it quickly roasted”. The oldest detailed recipe for Black Pudding is attributed to Apicius…

- Tomatoes

The word tomato, earlier tomate (c. 1600), come from Spanish tomate (mid-16c.) from Nahuatl (Aztecan) tomatl. The French gave it the name pomme d'amour; hence the English and early American term ‘love apple’. One early Italian writer called the tomato poma Peruviana, suggesting that it was introduced from Peru. Another called it poma d'oro, or "gold apple," indicating that the earliest introductions were yellow-fruited. The tomato is native to western South America…
